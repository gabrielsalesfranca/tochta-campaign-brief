Section 01

The market reality

To communicate effectively for Tochta, you need to understand the world the customer lives in — the RV lifestyle, the industry, and why this specific moment in time is an exceptional opportunity.

The recreational vehicle market in the United States is not a niche hobby. It is a mainstream lifestyle that spans generations, income levels, and motivations. As of 2024, 11.2 million American households own an RV — and nearly 17 million more express interest in owning one within five years. One in four leisure trips taken in the US today is a camping trip. RV camping reached nearly 10 million households in 2024, with total camping expenditures hitting $61 billion that year.

This is the world Tochta operates in. A massive, engaged, lifestyle-driven community of people who have made a significant financial and emotional investment in a way of living that centers on freedom, the outdoors, and time with family. They are not casual consumers. They are deeply committed to this lifestyle — and they upgrade, customize, and invest in their rigs constantly.

But here is the critical context that makes right now the most important moment for this brand: RV sales hit a historic record of over 600,000 units in 2021, driven by the pandemic travel boom. Millions of Americans who had never owned an RV before bought one. They were first-time buyers, many of them younger than the typical RV demographic, excited about road travel and outdoor living. They bought their rigs. They hit the road. And they have been sleeping on the factory mattress that came with it ever since.

That was three to five years ago. Factory RV mattresses are built from the cheapest possible foam — manufacturers spend as little as $80 on the mattress for a $60,000 to $100,000+ vehicle. That foam degrades quickly. Within one to two years, the support structure begins to fail. By year three or four, the sleeper is effectively lying on compressed foam with minimal support. Millions of those 2021 buyers are right now living with a mattress that is failing underneath them — and many of them haven't connected the dots between their morning back pain and the thing they're sleeping on.

This is the replacement wave. It is open right now. Tochta's job — and our job in creating this content — is to be the clear, credible, emotionally resonant answer the moment those buyers start searching for a solution.

Beyond the replacement wave, there are several other market trends that directly benefit Tochta's positioning right now.

The quality-over-quantity shift. Recent travel research shows that nearly two-thirds of RV travelers are planning fewer trips, but more meaningful, higher-quality ones. They are spending more per experience — not less. They are thinking harder about every element of their trip, from the destination to the gear. A premium mattress fits perfectly into this mindset shift. This is not the moment to lead with price. This is the moment to lead with quality, comfort, and the idea that the trip they've invested in deserves to be fully experienced.

Sleep health has become a cultural conversation. In a way it never was five years ago, consumers are now aware that sleep quality affects everything — mood, performance, physical health, emotional wellbeing, and the ability to enjoy life. The wellness industry has done much of the education work here. People now understand that poor sleep isn't just tiredness. Science confirms that poor sleep quality specifically reduces the intensity of positive emotions — the joy, the awe, the connection. The sunrise means less when you're exhausted. The hike feels worse. Tochta's product is now sitting at the intersection of the wellness conversation and the RV lifestyle, and both audiences are ready to hear this message.

The van life and digital nomad movement. A growing segment of the market — younger, remote-working, highly social-media active — has adopted van life and full-time RV living as a primary lifestyle. These buyers have non-standard sleeping spaces by definition, they document their lives online, and they are deeply community-driven. Tochta's custom sizing capability is uniquely suited to serve them, and their social media presence makes them natural UGC amplifiers.

The competitive landscape in one paragraph

Tochta competes in two arenas simultaneously. The first is the broad DTC mattress space — brands like Casper, Purple, Helix, and Saatva — which have large marketing budgets and brand recognition but cannot serve custom sizes or shapes. The second is the specialist RV/custom mattress space, where Wilderness RV Mattress is the primary threat. Wilderness is well-resourced, growing fast, and offers a hybrid/coil option that Tochta currently lacks. But Wilderness has no equivalent to the Divini's dual-zone firmness technology — that is Tochta's single most defensible competitive advantage. No other brand in this niche offers independent per-side firmness control. Combined with 25+ years of real factory ownership and a custom builder that generates instant quotes for any shape, Tochta holds a position in the market that no competitor can fully replicate.

Section 02

The product — what Tochta actually makes and why it matters

You cannot communicate this product well if you don't understand what it is, how it's made, and what problem each layer solves. This is not a mattress company with a clever brand. It is a real factory making a genuinely superior product. That difference must come through in every piece of content.

Tochta was founded by mattress factory owners who were also passionate RV travelers. They experienced firsthand the poor quality of factory-installed RV mattresses and decided to solve the problem themselves — from the manufacturing floor up. The company has owned and operated its factory in South Carolina for over 25 years. This is not marketing language. In a DTC mattress market full of brands that are essentially websites with outsourced production, Tochta is the real thing. They control every layer, every material, every quality decision. That institutional knowledge and manufacturing control is what makes the product genuinely better — and it is what makes the brand's credibility claims defensible.

Every Tochta mattress is CertiPUR-US certified — a rigorous foam safety standard that tests for harmful chemicals, emissions, and heavy metals. More importantly for the RV market, Tochta is explicitly fiberglass-free. This matters enormously. Budget mattresses — including many factory RV mattresses and cheap online alternatives — use fiberglass as a fire retardant hidden inside the foam. When disturbed or when the cover is removed, fiberglass can contaminate an entire space and cause serious skin and respiratory irritation. RV owner communities on forums and Reddit are increasingly aware of this problem, and Tochta's fiberglass-free certification is becoming a stronger purchase signal every month.

Section 03

The customers — who they are, what they feel, and what finally moves them

This is the most important section for anyone creating content. You need to understand these four people deeply — not as demographic profiles, but as human beings with specific frustrations, specific fears, and specific moments of decision. If you don't feel like you know them, read this section again.

A critical principle before we profile each persona: none of these people are shopping for a mattress when they first encounter Tochta. They are living with a problem they have partially normalized. They have told themselves "this is just how it is" and moved on. The job of our content is not to find people who are already in the market — it is to wake up the people who have accepted their situation and show them that it doesn't have to be this way.

This is the difference between demand capture and demand creation. Most ads capture demand that already exists. The best Tochta content creates demand by making people feel a pain they had stopped consciously noticing. That is a harder and more important job. It requires empathy, specificity, and the willingness to name things exactly.

01

The Weekend Wanderer

Couples, ages 45–65 · Suburban homeowners · $80K–$150K household income · 4–12 RV weekends per year

→ Utopia / Divini

This is the largest and most accessible persona for Tochta. They love their RV. It represents freedom, family time, and a particular kind of American life they've worked hard to afford. They've spent real money on the vehicle itself — often $50,000 to $150,000+ — and they use it regularly. But there's a quiet problem that follows every trip home: they feel worse than when they left. Their backs hurt. They're tired in a way that isn't just from driving. And they've started to wonder whether RV trips are actually restful at all.

The key psychological reality of this persona is normalization. They have accepted that sleeping in an RV means sleeping worse. They've tried to fix it: a $90 memory foam topper from Amazon, a gel pad from Costco, layering two toppers on top of each other. None of it solved the problem, because none of it addressed the actual issue — the degraded, inadequate foam base underneath. But they don't know that. They think the problem is with their bodies, their age, their sleeping habits.

The other defining feature of this persona is the couples dimension. In almost every case, there are two people sharing this mattress — and they have different needs. One sleeps hot and kicks the covers off all night. The other needs firmer support for lower back pain. One is a light sleeper disturbed by every movement. The other snores. They've been politely accommodating each other's discomfort for years, and it has become a source of low-grade tension that neither of them acknowledges openly. The Divini was specifically engineered to resolve this exact situation, and the Weekend Wanderer is the bull's-eye target for that product.

Emotional pain

They feel cheated. They invested heavily in this dream and the mattress is quietly undermining it. Every bad trip deepens the feeling that something is wrong but they can't quite name what. They blame themselves — "I just can't sleep away from home" — when the real culprit is the foam.

Rational pain

Waking up sore ruins the first hours of every trip day. They spend the first morning of a vacation stiff and irritable. Coffee helps a little. But the damage to the trip's mood is done before anyone sees the sunrise.

The hidden fear holding them back

"Custom means expensive and complicated." They assume ordering a custom mattress means phone calls, confusing measurements, long waits, and the risk of getting it wrong. The instant-quote builder exists to demolish this fear — but they have to see it to believe it.

What finally breaks them

One particularly bad trip — both wake up sore every single morning, someone says something on the drive home, and this time one of them actually searches for a solution instead of just agreeing to fix it later. That search is the moment Tochta must own.

The breaking point quote

"We just got back from two weeks in Yellowstone and I woke up sore every single morning. We spent $8,000 on that trip. I can't do this anymore."

02

The Full-Time RV Lifer

Remote workers & retirees, ages 35–65 · RV is primary or secondary full-time home · High motivation to optimize every element of the living space

→ Divini

This persona has made the biggest commitment. The RV is not a vacation vehicle — it is their home. They have thought carefully about every system in the rig: the solar setup, the water filtration, the kitchen appliances, the wifi situation, the storage configuration. They approach RV living the way a well-resourced person approaches home design. They invest in quality because they live with every decision every day.

The Full-Timer does deep research before buying anything. They read forums, watch YouTube reviews, compare specs, ask questions in Facebook groups. They are not impulse buyers, but when they decide, they spend confidently and without regret. Peer recommendations from other full-timers carry more weight than any advertising. A single trusted voice in r/RVliving or a full-timer's YouTube review of a Tochta mattress is worth more than months of paid ad spend with this audience.

The emotional tension for this persona is the gap between their commitment to the lifestyle and the one element that hasn't been upgraded yet. They've upgraded everything visible. The mattress is the last holdout — partly because it's inconvenient to deal with, partly because it requires measurement and customization, and partly because they've told themselves they can live with it. But they can't, and they know it. Sleeping on a degrading factory mattress 365 nights a year is not sustainable. At some point the calculus tips.

This persona also cares deeply about durability and longevity. They're not buying a mattress for weekends. They're buying it for their home. The 10-year warranty, the dense layered construction, and the 25-year factory history all matter enormously here. They need to know that this purchase will last, and they need to trust that the company behind it will still be there if something goes wrong.

Emotional pain

Their identity is tied to the lifestyle choice they made. A bad mattress feels like a crack in the dream — a signal that full-time RV life is less livable than they committed to. Fixing it is almost an act of loyalty to the choice they made.

Rational pain

365 nights a year on a degrading mattress. Chronic back pain accumulates differently when there's no "home bed" to recover on. They need real density, real support, and a product that won't need replacing in two years.

The hidden fear

"Is this company legitimate? Will they honor the warranty in year 7? Are they just another DTC brand with no real manufacturing behind them?" They have been burned by online brands before. Factory proof is not just reassuring — it is the deciding factor.

What finally breaks them

A specific forum post or video from another full-timer saying "I finally found it." This persona converts through community. A trusted peer recommendation in the right forum at the right time converts faster than any paid campaign.

The breaking point quote

"This is my house. I've upgraded everything about my life on the road. Why am I still sleeping on the mattress that came with the rig?"

03

The Long-Haul Trucker

Professional drivers, ages 30–60 · 3–5 nights per week in sleeper cab · Back pain and sleep deprivation are occupational realities

→ Journey / Utopia

The Long-Haul Trucker is in many ways the most underserved persona in the mattress market. Their sleeping situation is uniquely challenging: the sleeper cab of a commercial truck has completely non-standard dimensions, requires a mattress that can handle heavy daily use, and needs to provide genuine lumbar support for someone who has also been sitting for many hours a day. Bad sleep for a trucker is not just uncomfortable — it is a safety issue. Fatigue behind the wheel is one of the leading causes of serious commercial vehicle accidents.

This persona has deep and well-founded skepticism of marketing. They have heard "built for truckers" from brands that still only offer standard sizes. They have tried mattresses that don't fit, toppers that slide off overnight, and cheap foam pads that bottomed out within weeks. They have spent money solving this problem multiple times and it has not worked. When they encounter Tochta's custom builder and see that they can enter their exact cab dimensions and get a precise price immediately, the reaction is not excitement — it is relief mixed with cautious skepticism. The content challenge is convincing them that this is actually different.

This persona converts almost exclusively through peer recommendation. Driver communities — Facebook groups, trucking forums, conversations at truck stops — are the primary trust networks. Content that speaks directly and practically to their specific situation, uses the right language, and is backed by real driver testimonials has a chance. Generic lifestyle content does not reach them.

Emotional pain

They feel invisible. Nobody designs products for them specifically. RV mattress brands talk about camping trips. They need something built for occupational daily use in a non-standard space — and the industry treats them as an afterthought.

Rational pain

Cab mattress dimensions are wildly non-standard. Standard sizes don't fit. They've tried cut-down mattresses, folded foam, and stacked pads — all fail. The right size literally doesn't exist in any store. The custom builder is the only real answer.

The hidden fear

"This is just another brand that says 'fits most sleeper cabs.'" The custom builder is the proof, not the promise. Content should lead with the builder's ability to handle exact cab dimensions — not lifestyle imagery.

What finally breaks them

Another driver they trust recommends it. Or their back gets bad enough that they recognize this is an occupational health problem, not just discomfort. The "I can't keep doing this to my body" moment after a particularly bad run.

The breaking point quote

"I drive 300,000 miles a year and I sleep in this cab 5 nights a week. My back is wrecked. I need something that actually fits and actually works."

04

The Custom Home Sleeper

Homeowners, ages 28–55 · Non-standard frames, boat berths, lofts, Murphy beds, antique frames · Has already exhausted all standard solutions

→ Utopia / Divini

This persona is often overlooked in Tochta's marketing but represents a significant and highly motivated buyer segment. They are not RV owners — they are homeowners with a sleeping space that doesn't conform to standard mattress sizes. This includes people with antique bed frames that pre-date standardization, custom-built loft beds, Murphy beds in small apartments, boat berths and marine sleeping spaces, and any number of other non-standard configurations.

What makes this persona particularly interesting from a conversion standpoint is that they are already convinced they need a custom mattress. The persuasion work is essentially done before they arrive. They have been to every mattress store. They have measured three times. They have searched online for "custom mattress" and found confusing options with no transparent pricing and multi-week lead times. When they find Tochta's custom builder and see that they can enter their exact dimensions, choose their corner geometry, and get an instant price — without calling anyone — the reaction is almost immediate conversion intent. The friction for this persona is not persuasion. It is discovery and fear of measuring wrong.

Content for this persona should focus on demonstrating that the custom builder can handle their specific scenario. Showing unusual shapes being built — radius corners, notched cuts, hinged configurations, unusual widths — is the content that converts this buyer. They need to see their problem solved before they believe it can be.

Emotional pain

Years of living with a space that doesn't feel finished — a beautiful loft or custom bedroom undermined by a mattress that doesn't fit right. The gap, the overhang, the wrong dimensions have bothered them since day one. Relief is the dominant emotion when Tochta solves it.

Rational pain

Every workaround has failed. Cutting a standard mattress creates uneven sleeping surfaces. Two twin mattresses side by side create a gap. "Close enough" sizes are never close enough once you're sleeping on them every night.

The hidden fear

"What if I measure wrong and spend $700 on a mattress that doesn't fit?" This is the single biggest stall point. Content that demystifies the measuring process — with visuals, clear instructions, and reassurance about customer support if something goes wrong — is the conversion unlock.

What finally breaks them

Seeing the builder handle their exact case. Radius corners. Unusual widths. The visual confirmation that "yes, we can do that" for their specific problem triggers near-immediate purchase intent. Show the unusual case, not the standard one.

The breaking point quote

"I've been searching for two years. My bed frame is custom-built and nothing fits it. I can't believe there's actually a company that will build it to my exact measurements."

Section 04

The emotional strategy — why feeling comes before selling

This section explains the strategic framework that governs every piece of content in this campaign. Understanding why we communicate the way we do is as important as knowing what to say.

The most important strategic principle in this entire document is this: Tochta's customers are not buying a mattress. They are buying back the experience they already paid for. The RV cost $80,000. The trip was planned for months. The full-time life was a major life decision. The custom home was years in the making. And in each case, the one thing quietly undermining all of it is the thing they sleep on. The mattress is the last broken piece of a dream they've already committed to.

This reframing changes everything about how we communicate. If the product is "a better mattress," we are competing on specs and price with every other foam company on the internet. If the product is "the completion of a dream you've already invested in," we are in a completely different conversation — one that is emotionally resonant, personally relevant, and impossible for a cheaper competitor to win.

There is also hard science behind the deepest emotional argument in this campaign. Research published in the Journal of Sleep Research found that while negative emotions survive sleep deprivation relatively intact, positive feelings become significantly less intense when sleep quality is poor. This is not a minor effect. It means that the RV owner sleeping on a degrading factory mattress is not just tired when they wake up — they are literally less capable of feeling joy that day. The sunrise at the Grand Canyon hits less hard. The family dinner around the campfire is less funny. The hike registers with less wonder. Bad sleep doesn't just make you tired. It steals the feeling from the places you came all this way to see. That is the deepest emotional truth in this entire campaign, and it should inform the most powerful pieces of content.

The core strategic insight

"RV owners don't buy a mattress. They buy back the trip they already paid for."

Every piece of content in this campaign exists to make that truth visible to the right person at the right moment. Not to sell them a product, but to show them what they're losing by not having one.

The four emotional deliverables

These are the four things Tochta emotionally delivers to its customers — not product features, but felt outcomes. Every piece of content should connect to at least one of these. They are listed in strategic priority order.

Emotional Deliverable 01 — Highest priority

The completion of the dream

The customer has already made a major commitment — to the RV, to the road trip, to the lifestyle, to the home they built. They are not starting something new; they are trying to complete something they've already begun. The mattress is the last broken piece. When Tochta solves it, the customer isn't just buying comfort — they're closing the loop on an investment they care deeply about. This is why messaging that references the cost of the RV, the planning of the trip, or the commitment to the lifestyle is so effective. It validates the investment they've already made and positions Tochta as the thing that finally makes it whole.

Emotional proof line

"You didn't spend that much on your rig to wake up sore every morning."

Rational proof

25-year factory, CertiPUR-US certification, 10-year warranty, free shipping.

Emotional Deliverable 02

Permission to fully enjoy the moment

This is the sleep-science argument made emotional. The research is clear: poor sleep quality reduces the intensity of positive emotions. Tochta doesn't sell rest — it sells the ability to actually feel the places you're in. The view from the mountain. The family conversation at the campfire. The freedom of the open road. These experiences are what the customer is chasing — but they are emotionally inaccessible when you're exhausted. Tochta removes that barrier. Content built around this deliverable shows the contrast between what the experience is supposed to feel like and what it feels like when you're running on bad sleep. The visual language is landscapes and moments that should be transcendent, slightly dimmed by the fog of exhaustion.

Emotional proof line

"Wake up ready to actually be where you are."

Rational proof

Gel cooling layers, quick-recovery foam, progressive support system, latex bounce layer.

Emotional Deliverable 03

Peace between partners

Across all four personas, the single most commonly cited frustration is the couples' sleep conflict. One person runs hot. The other needs firmer support. One is a light sleeper. The other moves constantly. They have been silently accommodating each other's discomfort for years — it has become a low-grade source of tension that neither person acknowledges directly, but both feel. The Divini dual-zone is the only product in the RV or custom mattress market that directly and completely resolves this. No compromise needed. No one has to sacrifice their comfort for the other's. Both people win. This is not just a product benefit — it is a relationship benefit, and it should be communicated as such. Content for this deliverable should be intimate, knowing, and slightly playful — the tone of a brand that understands what it's like to be in this situation because they've seen it thousands of times.

Emotional proof line

"Finally — a mattress that doesn't make you negotiate your sleep."

Rational proof

9 firmness levels per side, 81 possible combinations, patent-pending dual-zone technology.

Emotional Deliverable 04

The end of compromise

Every other mattress brand in the world asks the customer to adapt to what's available. Tochta is the first and only brand in this space to reverse that dynamic: "Tell us exactly what you need and we'll build it." This is a fundamentally different emotional offer — not "here's what we have, pick one" but "what do you need? We'll make it." For the Custom Home Sleeper and the Trucker, this is the entire story. For every other persona, it is a powerful proof of commitment — a brand that respects the specificity of your situation enough to solve it exactly, not approximately. The custom builder is the physical manifestation of this deliverable. It should be demonstrated, not just described.

Emotional proof line

"Your RV is custom. Your mattress should be too."

Rational proof

Custom shape builder with instant quote, any dimension, angled/radius/notched options.

The message sequence — non-negotiable

The order in which we communicate matters as much as what we say. Lead with rational information and you lose the customer before they've felt anything. Lead with emotion, bridge to empathy, and close with rational proof — in that order, every time. This applies to every piece of content, from a single-frame Instagram ad to a five-slide carousel to a product page.

01

Hook — emotional

3 to 6 words. Speaks directly to the felt pain. No product, no features, no brand name. Pure recognition. The person should feel seen before being sold anything.

"You didn't come this far to sleep this bad."

02

Bridge — empathy

1 to 2 sentences. Names the real cause of the problem — factory foam economics, or the one-size-fits-all industry. Validates their frustration. Positions Tochta as someone who lived this and decided to fix it.

"Factory RV mattresses are built to a price point, not a sleep standard. We know — we used to sleep on them too."

03

Close — rational proof

3 to 5 facts that earn the trust the emotional hook created. Factory history, certifications, custom builder, trial period, warranty. Brief and specific — not a spec sheet.

"Built in our SC factory. Any size, any shape. 100-night trial. 10-year warranty."

What to never lead with

Never lead with specs. Never lead with price. Never use the word "premium" without showing what premium means — it has no meaning left. Never be vague about the pain — "better sleep" is too soft. Name the specific problem: morning back pain, waking up sore, too exhausted to enjoy the trip, the couple who needs different things. Specificity is the difference between content that stops the scroll and content that disappears into the feed.
